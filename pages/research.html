Research CV

Andrew Weldon

Ph.D. Candidate of Astrophysics

The formation and evolution of galaxies are shaped by the flow of baryons. Inflows of pristine gas from the intergalactic medium (IGM) to galaxies sustain their star formation and fuels blackhole growth. The energy output from massive stars and black holes, in turn, drives outflows of interstellar gas, regulating star formation, modulating the metallicity within galaxies, and enriching the IGM with metals. This baryon cycle transporting gas from the IGM through the circumgalactic medium (CGM) into galaxies and outflows from the interstellar medium (ISM) into the surrounding CGM and possibly beyond into the IGM plays a crucial role in galaxy evolution. In my research, I am tracing cold outflowing and inflowing gas from galaxies using low-ionization interstellar metal absorption lines in the rest-UV frame of z~2 star-forming galaxies at the peak of cosmic star formation.

Inflows

The accretion of pristine gas from the intergalactic medium (IGM) into galaxies is required to replenish their gas reservoir and sustain their star-formation throughout their evolution. While the basic theoretical predictions of accretion are well established, direct observations of accretion have remained notoriously difficult. Currently, I am investigating five star-forming galaxies at z~2 with detections of cold gas inflows. This project focuses on whether galaxies with detected inflows have different properties than galaxies with detected outflows and the covering fraction of inflowing gas.

Outflows

Galactic-scale outflows of gas from star-forming galaxies are a crucial component of galaxy evolution. However, the physical mechanisms that generate and sustain outflows remain an open question. We investigate the outflow velocities and physical properties of 155 zâˆ¼2 star-forming galaxies. We found that outflow velocities are marginally correlated with global SFR, but independent of SFR surface density (ΣSFR). The scaling between outflow velocity and SFR agrees with predictions of outflows driven by mechanical energy from supernovae explosions, rather than radiation pressure acting on dusty material. The relationship between outflow velocity and ΣSFR normalized by stellar mass (ΣsSFR) suggests that there is a threshold ΣsSFR, above which strong outflows are common regardless of ΣsSFR.

Mass-Metallicity Relation

The interstellar gas-phase metallicity of galaxies is the result of metal enrichment from star formation and the cycling of gas between galaxies and their environment. Measuring how metallicity evolves with stellar mass is a key test for our current understanding of physical processes that govern star formation in galaxies. In Weldon et al. (2020), we measured the mass-metallicity (MZ) relation in a sample of low-mass metal-poor galaxies at z~0.8. Using oxygen abundances measured with the Te method and robust stellar masses, we found that the z~0.8 MZ relation is similar to the local MZ relation, but offset towards lower metallicity at a given stellar mass, as expected. Additionally, we investigated whether the MZ relation has a secondary dependency on the SFR, such that the MZ relation is part of a larger non-evolving metallicity relation, but found no significant dependence on SFR.
