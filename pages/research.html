Andrew Weldon

Ph.D. Candidate of Astrophysics

The flow of baryons into and out of galaxies shapes their evolution. Inflows of pristine gas from the intergalactic medium (IGM) into galaxies sustain their star formation and fuels black hole growth. The energy output from massive stars and black holes, in turn, drives outflows of interstellar gas, regulating star formation, modulating the metallicity within galaxies, and enriching the surriunding environment with metals. This baryon cycle transporting gas from the IGM through the circumgalactic medium (CGM) into galaxies and outflows from the interstellar medium (ISM) into the surrounding CGM and possibly beyond into the IGM plays a crucial role in galaxy evolution. In my dissertation research, I focus on characterizing galactic-scale outflows from star-forming galaxies during cosmic noon (z ~ 1 ‚Äì 3, the peak of the cosmic star-formation history). My research utilizes the extensive spectroscopic dataset of the MOSFIRE Deep Evolution Field survey (MOSDEF) with additional deep observations from the Keck Low Resolution Imaging Spectrometer (LRIS) and integral field (IFU) spectroscopy from the Keck Cosmic Web Imager (KCWI). This combination of strong rest-optical emission and rest-UV absorption lines creat an ideal dataset to investigate both the warm ionized (~104 K) and cool neutral (~103 K) gas outflows on an individual galaxy basis.

Inflows

The accretion of pristine gas from the intergalactic medium into galaxies is required to replenish their gas reservoir and sustain their star-formation throughout their evolution. While the basic theoretical predictions of accretion are well established, direct observations of accretion have remained sparse. In Weldon et al. 2023, we present three star-forming galaxies at ùëß ‚àº 2 with robust measurements of redshifted low-ionization absorption metal lines tracing inflowing gas. The detection of three galaxies with significant inflows from a larger spectroscopic sample of 131 galaxies implies a small inflow covering fraction of ~2%. Galaxies with detected inflows and redward-skewed absorption line profiles do not show unique galactic properties relative to other galaxies. Additionally, within the sample, the covering fraction of inflowing gas decreases rapidly with increasing velocity, such that most inflowing gas is masked by more prevalent outflowing gas. Together, these results imply that the low detection rate of galaxies with inflows is primarily related to the small solid angle subtended by inflowing gas and our viewing angle rather than the physical properties of the galaxies.

Outflows

Galactic-scale outflows of gas from star-forming galaxies are a crucial component of galaxy evolution. However, the physical mechanisms that generate and sustain outflows remain an open question. In Weldon et al. (2022), we investigate relations betwen galactic properties and outflow velocities ‚Äì traced by low-ionization interstellar metal absorption lines ‚Äì of 155 star-forming galaxies z ‚àº 2 to constrain the primary driving mechanism of their outflows. We found that outflow velocities are marginally correlated with global SFR, but independent of SFR surface density (ΣSFR). The scaling between outflow velocity and SFR agrees with predictions of outflows driven by mechanical energy from supernovae explosions, rather than radiation pressure acting on dusty material. Additionally, the relationship between outflow velocity and ΣSFR normalized by stellar mass (ΣsSFR) suggests that there is a threshold ΣsSFR, above which strong outflows are common regardless of ΣsSFR.

Mass-Metallicity Relation

The interstellar gas-phase metallicity of galaxies is the result of metal enrichment from star formation and the cycling of gas between galaxies and their environment. Measuring how metallicity evolves with stellar mass is a key test for our current understanding of physical processes that govern star formation in galaxies. In Weldon et al. (2020), we measured the mass-metallicity (MZ) relation in a sample of low-mass metal-poor galaxies at z~0.8. Using oxygen abundances measured with the Te method and robust stellar masses, we found that the z~0.8 MZ relation is similar to the local MZ relation, but offset towards lower metallicity at a given stellar mass, as expected. Additionally, we investigated whether the MZ relation has a secondary dependency on the SFR, such that the MZ relation is part of a larger non-evolving metallicity relation, but found no significant dependence on SFR.
